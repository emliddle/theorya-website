Theory A

A new theory for a new mode of work.

Every major shift in how value is created has required organizations to rethink how they are designed and managed. AI represents another such shift. Capabilities that were once scarce are becoming abundant. Expertise can scale differently. And the value of distinctly human capabilities -- judgment, imagination, discernment, and agency -- is changing.

Theory A begins with a question: how should we design organizations when both humans and machines can act?

The answer requires more than AI adoption. It requires a new theory of the organization.

The Principles of Theory A

Five principles. One connected system.

The enduring questions at the center of Theory A.

Theory A begins with a different set of questions.

Most management practices were built for a world of scarcity: limited intelligence, limited expertise, limited capacity, and high coordination costs. As those conditions change, the assumptions beneath the organization must be questioned too.

These five principles are the lenses through which we do that work. They help us examine what has become abundant, how agency should be orchestrated, what outcomes the organization exists to produce, what form enables people and AI to act, and whether the system becomes more capable through use.

They are not a methodology or a sequence to follow. They are a connected system for questioning, designing, and continually improving how organizations work.

The Principles of Theory A

Abundance

What economic reality are you designing for?

When previously scarce capabilities become abundant, economic value shifts. Every mode of production changes which constraints matter. Organizations designed around yesterday's scarcity will optimize the wrong things. The first design question is not what AI can do. It is what has now become the real constraint.

Orchestration

How are different forms of agency designed to work together?

The challenge is no longer human adoption of technology. Organizations must deliberately orchestrate human and artificial agency: determining where each acts independently, where they collaborate, where judgment must remain human, and how action stays aligned to shared purpose. Substitution is the wrong frame. Orchestration is the right one.

Outcome

What is the organization fundamentally organizing around?

Agency creates value when it is directed toward meaningful outcomes. Organizations designed around roles, functions, and inherited boundaries can fragment action. Theory A organizes capability around outcomes and gives people and systems the context and authority to act toward them.

Form

What structures create or constrain agency?

Structure is not neutral. Decision rights, information flows, incentives, technology, governance, and boundaries determine where agency can emerge and whether distributed action becomes coordinated capability. Their architecture either serves that purpose or quietly works against it.

Compounding

Does the system increase or dissipate organizational capability over time?

Organizations either compound agency or dissipate it. Every structural decision does one or the other. A Theory A organization learns through action: experience strengthens judgment, knowledge becomes reusable, digital capability improves, and each cycle leaves the organization more capable than the one before.
